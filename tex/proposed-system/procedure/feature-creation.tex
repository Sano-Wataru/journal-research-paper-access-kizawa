We carry out image-based feature modeling as the first method.
This method generates image arrays from frames of time-series point-cloud data and converts them into statistics of similarity changes between the image arrays.
The hypothesis of this method is that changes in a person's overall physical movement affect activity levels.
Such changes can be measured as changes in the similarity of point-cloud frames \cite{kizawa2023estimation}.

The method follows a specific process for feature modeling.
We convert 3D point-cloud data into two-dimensional (2D) information for each frame.
We project each point in the point-cloud data onto a plane formed by two of the three coordinates ($x$, $y$, and $z$), as shown in Fig.~\ref{trans3dto2d}.
We carry out the projection on all three axes for all frames.
We then convert a 3D point-cloud frame into a 2D image and generate a time-series 2D pixel array.
We refer to the time-series 2D pixel array as a `video frame' going forward.
We calculate a similarity between frames of the video frame and use zero-mean normalized cross-correlation (ZNCC) as a measure of image similarity, which is commonly used for measuring image similarity, particularly in template matching \cite{chen2020self,NAKHMANI2013315}.
ZNCC is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:zncc}
  \mathrm{ZNCC} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{MN} (x_{i}-\bar{x})(y_{i}-\bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{MN} (x_{i}-\bar{x})^2 \sum_{i=1}^{MN} (y_{i}-\bar{y})^2}},
\end{equation}
where $x$ and $y$ are the intensity values of the 1st and 2nd dimensions, and $M$ and $N$ are the number of pixels of 1st and 2nd dimensions, respectively.
We derive the ZNCC between each of the video frames.
We then obtain the ZNCC between the $i$-th and $i+1$-th frames for the 1st to $I-1$-th frames, where $I$ is the number of frames.
We obtain a distribution of ZNCC from the video frame.
We extract the 90th and 50th percentile values from the distribution of the obtained ZNCC scores as numerical features.
The 50th percentile value is selected to reflect the central tendency of the feature distribution in a manner that is minimally influenced by outliers.
The 90th percentile value is selected to capture upper-range variations that are not fully represented by the central tendency alone.

We carry out centroid feature modeling as a second method.
This method converts a head region of time-series point-cloud data into statistics of center-of-gravity changes.
The hypothesis of this method is that changes in the center of gravity of the head affect activity levels.
The method follows a specific process for feature modeling.
We extract a set of 3D coordinates from an initial frame that represents a human head, define the 3D coordinates as a head region, extract the head region from all frames, and calculate the sum of each coordinate in the head region for each frame.
Each frame is represented as a set of $n$ points ($x_{i}$, $y_{i}$, $z_{i}$) in 3D coordinate space.
We define the sum of the $x$, $y$, and $z$ for each point as
\begin{equation}
  \left\{
  \begin{aligned}
    X &= \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i},\\
    Y &= \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_{i},\\
    Z &= \sum_{i=1}^{n} z_{i}.
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
\end{equation}
We calculate the center of gravity for all up to frame $I$.
The center of gravity $G_{j}$ can be expressed as the sum of each coordinate divided by the total number of points $n$:
\begin{equation}
  G_{j} = \left(\frac{X}{n}, \frac{Y}{n}, \frac{Z}{n}\right),
\end{equation}
where $j$ represents the frame number, ranging from the 1st frame to the $I$-th frame.
We calculate the difference between the central point of the center of gravity of all frames and the center of gravity of each frame.
We define the central point $C$ of the center of gravity of all frames as
\begin{equation}
  C = \frac{1}{I}\sum_{j=1}^{I} G_{j}.
\end{equation}
We represent the difference as Euclidean distance \cite{blais1995registering}.
We obtain a distribution of the differences from the head region of time-series point-cloud data and extract the 90th and 50th percentile values from the distribution of the differences in center-of-gravity changes.
